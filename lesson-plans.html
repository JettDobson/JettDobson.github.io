Lesson and Unit Design

This section focuses on how I approach planning and the structures I use to organize instruction. My planning begins with the Ontario curriculum, where I identify the overall and specific expectations that need to be addressed, then work backward to develop learning goals, success criteria, assessment opportunities, and instructional sequences. Rather than focusing only on subject-specific artifacts, this section highlights the planning processes, templates, and documents that guide my preparation across teaching contexts.

My Approach to Unit Planning

Unit planning asks me to think beyond single lessons and consider how learning develops over time. When I plan a unit, I begin with the curriculum and the resources available to me. I use curriculum documents, textbooks, and other supports to shape the sequence of learning. From there, I think about how concepts connect and where students may need consolidation. I also try to balance direct instruction with inquiry, discussion, problem solving, labs, and practice. This helps create a unit that is varied, engaging, and responsive to different learning needs. Pacing is another important part of my planning. I try to build in flexibility, include buffer time, and avoid plans that feel too rigid. I also think about the rhythm of the week when organizing lessons and assessments. Some days may be better suited for heavier instruction, while others may support review, reflection, or hands-on work. This helps make the unit more realistic and manageable in an actual classroom. At the same time, unit planning is still an area where I am building experience. Because of that, I also value feedback from peers when developing a unit plan. Their insight helps me think more carefully about pacing, workload, and whether the plan feels reasonable and teachable. This reminds me that strong planning is not only about organization. It is also about reflection, collaboration, and being willing to adjust ideas to better support student learning.

How I Approach Lesson Planning

When planning individual lessons, I begin by identifying what students need to know, understand, and be able to do by the end of the lesson. From there, I work to develop clear learning goals, appropriate success criteria, and a lesson flow that helps students move from introduction and modelling toward guided practice, application, and reflection. My lesson planning is informed by curriculum documents, formative assessment, professional feedback, class context, textbooks for content support, and conversations with others. I also draw on ideas from coursework, mentor input, and other teaching resources when thinking about how to structure learning in a way that is clear, engaging, and accessible. In practice, I want my lessons to balance explicit instruction with opportunities for discussion, guided practice, problem solving, hands-on learning, and student participation. I see lesson planning as both an organizational tool and a thinking process that helps me prepare to respond to learners more effectively.

Assessment

This section highlights how I understand and use assessment as an essential part of teaching and learning. I view assessment not only as a way to measure student progress, but also as a way to guide instruction, support reflection, and help students build a clearer understanding of their own learning. The artifacts included here are drawn primarily from coursework and reflect the assessment approaches, tools, and practices I am developing for classroom use. Assessment examples that were implemented directly during practicum are included in other sections of this portfolio where they are connected to specific lessons, placements, and teaching contexts. In my practice, assessment is most valuable when it is purposeful, ongoing, and connected directly to learning goals and success criteria.

How I Understand Assessment in Practice

I understand assessment as an essential part of the learning process rather than something that happens only after instruction is finished. In my practice, assessment is most effective when it is connected closely to learning goals, success criteria, and the day-to-day decisions that shape teaching. It helps make student thinking visible, provides evidence of understanding, identifies misconceptions, and supports more responsive instruction. This understanding is also informed by Ontario’s Growing Success document, which emphasizes that assessment should be fair, transparent, equitable, and supportive of student learning. That perspective is important to me because it reinforces that assessment is not simply about producing marks, but about using evidence in purposeful ways to support growth, communicate achievement clearly, and help students understand what quality learning looks like. One of the most important ideas that has shaped my thinking is the distinction between assessment as learning, assessment for learning, and assessment of learning. I see assessment as learning as especially important for helping students develop metacognition. When students are asked to reflect on what they understand, what they are still working on, and what strategies are helping them succeed, they become more aware of themselves as learners. This matters because students benefit from learning how to monitor their own progress, recognize areas of difficulty, and take greater ownership of next steps. In this sense, formative opportunities such as self-assessment, reflection, goal setting, and confidence checks are valuable not only because they generate information, but because they help students become more active and thoughtful participants in their own learning. I understand assessment for learning as the ongoing use of evidence to inform teaching before learning is finalized. This is especially important in practice because it allows me to respond to students in the moment rather than waiting until the end of a unit to discover where difficulties emerged. Observations, conversations, exit tickets, class discussions, checkpoints, and diagnostic tasks can all help reveal what students understand, where confusion remains, and what adjustments may be needed in pacing, modelling, or support. For me, this is one of the most valuable functions of assessment because it directly informs my own instructional practice. It allows me to make more responsive decisions, refine lessons, and better match instruction to the needs of the students in front of me. I understand assessment of learning as the evaluation of student achievement at a given point in time. This may include tests, performance tasks, projects, or other summative forms of assessment used to determine how well students have met expectations. While this type of assessment is important for reporting and communicating achievement, I do not see it as separate from the broader learning process. Its quality depends on strong alignment with curriculum expectations, learning goals, success criteria, and the instruction that came before it. When designed well, assessment of learning should provide a clear, fair, and meaningful picture of what students know and can do. Overall, I see assessment as deeply connected to planning, instruction, student reflection, and professional judgment. Effective assessment is not only about measuring learning. It is about supporting learning, improving teaching, and helping students develop a clearer understanding of themselves as learners. That is why I value assessment practices that are ongoing, intentional, and rooted in evidence, reflection, and responsiveness.

Assessment Example: Human Scatter Plot

One assessment example that has shaped my thinking is the human scatter plot, which I used in my coursework and also connected to my teachable area in physics. I value this activity because it makes student thinking visible in an active and low-pressure way while also giving me immediate insight into student confidence, understanding, and possible misconceptions. In my Physics Curriculum and Instruction course, I ran this activity by first giving students a short review of the concept, then providing each student with a question. I set up the classroom as a physical graph and asked students to place themselves on the graph according to the answer they selected and their comfort level with that answer. In this way, students were not only choosing a response, but also showing how confident they felt in their thinking. This activity is valuable because it allows assessment to happen through movement, discussion, and visible patterns in student responses. It gives students an opportunity to reflect on their own thinking while also allowing me to quickly identify trends, uncertainty, and areas that may need clarification before moving forward with instruction.

Written Reflection

The Human Scatterplot activity was one of my first meaningful opportunities to explore formative assessment in a classroom-focused setting. Although I first taught it to fellow teacher candidates in my Physics curriculum and instruction course, it still gave me valuable insight into how a formative assessment strategy can work in practice. By having students show both their answer and their level of confidence through movement and positioning, the activity made student thinking visible in a way that was active, engaging, and low pressure. What stood out to me most was how effectively this strategy can gather rich evidence of learning while still feeling accessible and enjoyable for students. Because students physically place themselves on a line or graph, the activity encourages participation, adds movement, and creates a simple entry point for all learners. At the same time, it allows the teacher to quickly notice patterns in understanding, confidence, and possible misconceptions. This helped me see more clearly how formative assessment can support both assessment for learning and assessment as learning, since it provides useful evidence to guide instruction while also encouraging students to reflect on their own understanding. This strategy became even more meaningful to me because I later used a version of it during my practicum with Grade 6/7 students. In that setting, I had all students respond to the same question and place themselves along a line based on their thinking. It worked well because it was low floor, movement-based, and engaging for younger learners, while also allowing me to quickly make note of how comfortable students were with the content. I found that it created a fun and supportive way to collect evidence of learning without making assessment feel difficult or intimidating. Overall, this experience strengthened my understanding that assessment can be both purposeful and engaging, and that some of the most useful evidence comes from strategies that make student thinking visible.

Assessment is an area of my practice that I have started to understand in a much more connected and purposeful way. Rather than seeing it only as something used to assign marks, I now see it as an ongoing part of teaching that helps make student thinking visible, guides instructional decisions, and creates greater clarity for both students and teacher. The examples included here reflect my growing understanding of how inquiry-based learning can deepen engagement, strengthen reasoning, and support scientific literacy by helping students interpret evidence, think critically, and make sense of ideas through investigation. Several of these examples were developed through collaborative course-based work, which also strengthened my understanding of how shared planning can improve lesson quality, clarity, and responsiveness to student needs.

Understanding and Value of Inquiry-Based, Problem-Based, and Project-Based Learning

Inquiry-based, problem-based, and project-based learning are closely related approaches that position students as active participants in the learning process rather than passive recipients of information. Across all three, students are asked to question, investigate, interpret information, apply knowledge, and communicate their thinking in purposeful ways. What makes these approaches especially valuable is that they move learning beyond the simple completion of fixed steps and instead encourage students to think critically, make decisions, justify conclusions, and build understanding through evidence, discussion, and reflection. A Venn diagram depicting the relationship among inquiry-based, problem-based, and project-based learning can be seen in Figure 1. At the same time, these approaches are not identical, and understanding their distinctions is important for thoughtful instructional planning. Inquiry-based learning is often driven by questions, exploration, and investigation, with an emphasis on helping students develop understanding through observation, reasoning, and evidence. Problem-based learning places students in a meaningful challenge or situation that requires analysis, decision-making, and justification as students work toward a response or solution. Project-based learning often extends over a longer period of time and typically asks students to create, design, or produce something that demonstrates their understanding in an applied and authentic way. Although each approach has a different emphasis, all three can make learning more active, meaningful, and connected to real contexts. I value these approaches because they can strengthen engagement while also supporting deeper thinking and scientific literacy. They give students opportunities to interpret evidence, ask thoughtful questions, analyze information, justify their reasoning, and communicate ideas with greater clarity. In science especially, this is important because students need opportunities not only to learn scientific concepts, but also to think in ways that reflect scientific reasoning and evidence-based decision-making. In mathematics and broader STEM contexts, these approaches also support persistence, problem solving, collaboration, and the transfer of understanding across different situations. This area has also contributed to my own professional growth because several of the examples in this section were developed collaboratively through coursework. As someone still early in my development as an educator, opportunities to collaborate with others are especially valuable because they allow me to see different perspectives on planning, questioning, task design, and student support. Engaging with those perspectives helps me think more carefully about my own choices and strengthens my ability to design learning experiences that are clearer, more purposeful, and more responsive to student needs. It has also helped me better understand that strong inquiry-, problem-, and project-based learning do not simply happen because a task is open-ended. They require careful planning, thoughtful structure, and purposeful guidance so that students are supported in investigating ideas, thinking deeply, and making meaningful sense of their learning.

Differentiation and Inclusive Practice

This section highlights how I work to create learning environments that are accessible, responsive, and inclusive. In my practice, differentiation is not about creating entirely separate classrooms within one room. It is about planning with learner variability in mind from the beginning so that more students can participate meaningfully in the same learning experience. I see inclusive practice as closely connected to lesson design, classroom environment, assessment, and the everyday decisions a teacher makes to support student access, confidence, and belonging.

How I Understand Differentiation and Inclusive Practice

I understand differentiation and inclusive practice as closely connected parts of effective teaching. Differentiation means planning intentionally for the range of learners in a classroom rather than waiting to respond only after students are already experiencing difficulty. This includes thinking about readiness, pace, background knowledge, confidence, learning preferences, and the different ways students may access and demonstrate understanding. In my practice, differentiation can include varied entry points, scaffolded instructions, flexible grouping, visual supports, choice in how students engage with a task, and opportunities for extension. I want students to be able to enter learning with confidence while still being challenged appropriately. Inclusive practice means designing learning environments where students feel respected, supported, and able to participate meaningfully. This includes considering IEP accommodations, accessibility needs, classroom routines, relationship-building, and the representation of different learner needs within planning. I want students to see the classroom as a place where support is normal, multiple approaches are valued, and learning is not limited to one format. Inclusion is strengthened when teaching is flexible, expectations are clear, and students are given the tools they need to access the same big ideas in different ways.

Understanding the Value of Collaborative Learning

This paper from my Physics Curriculum and Instruction course reflects a literature-based synopsis I completed to deepen my understanding of collaborative learning as a purposeful instructional approach rather than simply placing students in groups. In the article, the strategy of peer discussion combined with isomorphic problems was used to support Grade 11 students’ learning in kinematics and Newton’s Laws, with particular attention to conceptual understanding and graph interpretation. The study showed that students in the peer discussion group demonstrated stronger gains than those in the traditional instruction group, suggesting that structured discussion can help students move beyond formula use toward a deeper understanding of physics concepts. One of the most important ideas I took from this work is that collaborative learning becomes valuable when students are required to explain, justify, question, and revise their thinking with others. In physics especially, this matters because students can often complete calculations without fully understanding the concepts behind them. Peer discussion helps interrupt that pattern by making students commit to an answer, hear alternative reasoning, and think through why an idea does or does not make sense. I also think this is powerful because students are often able to understand the language and explanations of their peers in ways that feel more accessible than teacher explanations alone. When students explain concepts to one another, they often use simpler wording, shared points of confusion, and examples that make the learning feel more immediate and understandable. This work also helped me think more carefully about the conditions that make collaboration meaningful. Productive collaborative learning requires structure, thoughtful grouping, purposeful task design, and teacher guidance. Without that structure, group work can easily become uneven or superficial. With it, collaborative learning can support both academic growth and the development of communication, listening, and shared problem-solving skills that are important across subject areas. This synopsis also strengthened my understanding that collaborative learning needs to be structured carefully in order to be meaningful. The article highlighted that productive collaboration does not happen automatically. It depends on thoughtful task design, clear expectations, and teacher guidance so that discussion remains focused on reasoning rather than just sharing answers. This is something I try to carry into my own practice. In most of the lessons I create, I aim to incorporate a collaborative element somewhere in the learning process, whether through partner discussion, small-group problem solving, or shared reflection, and this can be seen throughout the lesson plans included in my portfolio. More broadly, this artifact reinforced my belief that collaborative learning is an important part of effective teaching because it helps students build conceptual understanding, develop communication skills, and become more active participants in their own learning.

Group Formation and Conditions for Effective Collaboration

This example reflects an important practical consideration in my approach to collaborative learning: meaningful collaboration depends not only on the quality of the task, but also on how groups are formed and supported. Through my practicum experience, I became more aware that grouping students is never a neutral decision. The way students are paired or placed together can strongly influence their confidence, level of participation, and overall success within a collaborative task. As a result, I have come to see group formation as an important part of instructional planning rather than something that should be decided casually in the moment. Because of this, I often prefer to create groups intentionally rather than relying only on random grouping. While random grouping can sometimes be useful, it can also create situations in which certain students feel left out, uncomfortable, or less able to participate fully. In some cases, it may unintentionally isolate a student socially or place them in a group dynamic that makes collaboration more difficult. Being intentional with grouping allows me to think carefully about how students work together, how they communicate, and what type of environment will best support respectful and productive learning. It also helps me ensure that no student is overlooked or placed at a disadvantage as a result of how the groups are formed. My practicum also helped me recognize that there are times when students should have some voice in grouping decisions, particularly when this supports their learning in a meaningful way. One example that stood out to me involved a student who had difficulty with reading and often felt more comfortable working with a close friend who could help read instructions and scribe ideas during shared tasks. Pairing those students together created a more supportive entry point into the activity and allowed the student to participate with greater confidence. This experience reinforced for me that thoughtful grouping is not only about classroom management or productivity, but also about responsiveness, inclusion, and creating conditions in which students can access learning more successfully. This has shaped the way I think about collaborative learning more broadly. Effective grouping requires me to consider a range of factors, including student confidence, relationships, communication styles, work habits, and support needs. It does not mean that students should always work with the same peers, nor does it mean that challenge and flexibility are unimportant. Rather, it means that grouping decisions should be made with purpose and adjusted when needed to reflect the realities of the classroom and the needs of the learners within it. Overall, this aspect of my practice has strengthened my understanding that collaborative learning works best when it is supported by thoughtful teacher decision-making. By planning groups intentionally, remaining responsive to student needs, and recognizing when student voice can play a role in that process, I can help build a classroom environment in which collaborative learning is more inclusive, productive, and genuinely beneficial.

Reflection on Collaborative Learning and P.E.O.E.

The Predict, Explain, Observe, Explain activity helped deepen my understanding of how collaborative learning can support student thinking in a meaningful way. In this activity, students worked in groups of three and used a whiteboard to record and discuss their ideas throughout the process. What I value about this structure is that it encourages students to make their thinking visible from the beginning, revisit it after observation, and then refine their understanding based on what they noticed. This creates a strong opportunity for learning because students are not simply trying to arrive at the correct answer. They are asked to explain their reasoning, listen to others, and reconsider their ideas as new evidence emerges. One of the most important takeaways for me was how much meaningful discussion this kind of task can generate when it is structured well. Working in small groups gave students space to share different perspectives, question one another, and explain their thinking in ways that were active and collaborative. Using whiteboards also supported this process by making ideas easy to record, revise, and talk through together. Activities like this remind me that collaborative learning is most effective when students are working with one another to make sense of ideas rather than simply dividing up work. It allows them to encounter different ways of thinking, explain concepts in their own words, and build stronger understanding through discussion. More broadly, this activity reinforced for me that collaborative learning can be a powerful way to support engagement, reasoning, and deeper conceptual understanding when students are given a clear structure and a meaningful task to work through together.

Classroom Management and Learning Environment

This section highlights my developing understanding of classroom management as an important part of effective teaching. At this stage in my growth as an educator, I see classroom management as closely connected to routines, relationships, clear expectations, and the overall learning environment. Rather than viewing it only as behaviour correction, I understand it as the ongoing work of creating a classroom that is structured, respectful, supportive, and conducive to learning.

My Developing Understanding of Classroom Management

My understanding of classroom management has grown through coursework, observation, and practicum experience. Early in teacher education, it can be easy to think of classroom management mainly in terms of addressing off-task behaviour or maintaining control. Over time, I have come to understand it in a broader and more meaningful way. Strong classroom management begins with planning, consistency, and the development of a classroom environment where students know what is expected and feel supported in meeting those expectations. I see classroom management as closely tied to instructional design and relationship-building. The way a lesson is structured, how clearly instructions are communicated, how materials are organized, and how transitions are handled can all shape student engagement and behaviour. In that sense, classroom management is not separate from teaching. It is part of how teaching is planned and carried out. A well-structured lesson, clear routines, and purposeful expectations can help prevent confusion and support a more productive learning environment. I also believe that effective classroom management should protect student dignity. Students benefit from clear boundaries and accountability, but they also benefit from being treated with care, patience, and respect. I want my classroom presence to be calm, steady, and consistent so that students experience structure without feeling controlled. This means working to create an environment where expectations are clear, support is available, and students are encouraged to take responsibility for their actions as part of a respectful learning community.

What I Am Learning to Prioritize

One important idea I have taken from my early experiences is that classroom management is often most effective when it is proactive rather than reactive. This includes establishing routines, communicating expectations clearly, designing lessons with appropriate pacing, and thinking ahead about how students will move through tasks. It also includes being consistent in how expectations are reinforced so that students experience the classroom as predictable and fair. I am also learning that classroom management involves helping students build independence and self-regulation. Rather than relying only on constant correction, I want to create classroom conditions where students are encouraged to be mindful of their choices, reflect on how their actions affect others, and contribute positively to the learning environment. This is an area I will continue developing, but it is already shaping how I think about teaching and classroom leadership.

How This Connects to My Practice

In my practice, this developing understanding influences how I think about lesson flow, transition planning, student accountability, and classroom climate. It reminds me that classroom management is built through many small decisions, including how I give directions, how I respond to disruptions, how I organize learning, and how I build relationships with students over time. Because I am still early in my development, I see this area as one of ongoing growth. My goal is to continue building a classroom presence that is warm, clear, and consistent while developing strategies that support both student responsibility and a positive learning environment.

Reflection

This area of learning has helped me understand that classroom management is not simply about responding when problems arise. It is about creating the conditions for learning from the beginning through structure, clarity, consistency, and relationships. One of the most important shifts in my thinking has been moving away from seeing classroom management as separate from instruction. I now see it as something embedded in planning, communication, routines, and the tone a teacher sets each day. As a beginning teacher candidate, I know this is an area where I am still developing through experience and reflection. At the same time, I have already learned that students respond well when expectations are clear, when routines are predictable, and when they feel respected by the adults in the room. Going forward, I want to continue strengthening my ability to balance care with accountability and to build a classroom environment where students can feel both supported and responsible for their learning.
